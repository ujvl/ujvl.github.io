Overview

I study the role that trustworthy computation can and should play in organizing a more free, just and equitable society. To this end, I'm currently exploring how tools from CS and economics—such as algorithmic game theory, social choice theory, cryptography and trusted hardware—can be practically leveraged to cultivate trust, redistribute power and improve the functioning of democratic organizations/institutions. I have also lately been grappling with the question of how to generally ground this work more formally in (virtue) ethics.

I'm fortunate to be advised by Dawn Song, and am affiliated with RDI & IC3. I'm physically based in NY at Cornell Tech, where I collaborate closely with Ari Juels and others.

Collaboration. Please reach out by email if you're a researcher or practitioner working in relevant areas! I'm particularly interested in hearing from those with expertise in disciplines other than my own (philosophy, political science, economics, sociology, psychology, other social sciences, humanities, arts etc). Happy to talk, collaborate and/or extend resources from my network in support of aligned causes. Be advised: in my excitement I suffer from a tendency to overcommit and spread myself thin (working on fixing this in therapy).

Background. I've spent several years meandering through various subfields in my quest for purpose (this may explain why my google scholar looks like a random walk). I had my start doing research at the AMPLab/RISELab building scalable distributed systems for applications in network systems and machine learning. Over time, however, I became increasingly weary of techno-optimist fervor in AI/ML, and decided to shift my focus towards security & privacy. In particular, I've spent the last couple years studying and designing decentralized/blockchain-based systems (both in my research and at my startup). This has brought me into contact with a broad range of techniques from the intersection of CS and economics, including those from algorithmic game theory and social choice theory. It has also convinced me that there is a dire need in CS for more ethics-driven thinking.

The flip side of all this breadth is that I (currently) lack the depth I want to have in many of these areas. But I have managed to surround myself with great collaboraters to mostly make up for that. And I'll eventually narrow my agenda down once I figure out how everything fits together better.

Future work. Other things I have a deep interest in but have yet to write about: CS x ethics education, misaligned incentives in academia, the dominance of utilitarian and meritocratic thinking in our culture... and more.

Outside work. avid reader, boxer (cal boxing '18), photog. warriors basketball fan. housing/transit policy enthusiast. I also occassionally help some friends run a local Indian food popup—you can help by following @curryinahurrynyc!
