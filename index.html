Overview

I study the role that trustworthy computation can and should play in organizing a more free, just and equitable society. To this end, I'm currently exploring how tools from particular areas in CS and economics—such as algorithmic game theory, computational social choice, trusted hardware, and cryptography—can be practically leveraged to cultivate trust, redistribute power and improve the functioning of democratic organizations/institutions. I have also lately been thinking about how to ground this work more formally in (virtue) ethics.

I'm fortunate to be advised by Dawn Song, and am affiliated with RDI & IC3. I'm physically based in NY at Cornell Tech, where I collaborate closely with Ari Juels and others.

Collaboration. Please reach out by email if you're a researcher or practitioner working in relevant areas! I'm particularly interested in hearing from those with expertise in disciplines other than my own (philosophy, political science, sociology, economics, psychology, history, literature etc). Happy to talk, collaborate and/or extend resources from my network in support of aligned causes. Be advised: in my excitement I suffer from a tendency to overcommit and spread myself thin (working on fixing this in therapy).

Background. I've spent several years wandering through various areas in search for purpose (this may explain why my google scholar looks like a random walk). I had my start doing research at the AMPLab/RISELab building scalable distributed systems for applications in network systems and machine learning. Over time, however, I became increasingly weary of techno-optimist fervor in AI/ML, and decided to shift my focus towards security & privacy. In particular, I've spent the last couple years studying and designing decentralized / blockchain-based systems (both in research and at my startup). This has brought me into contact with a broad range of techniques at the intersection of CS and economics, including those from algorithmic game theory and social choice theory. It has also convinced me that there is a dire need in CS for more rigorous ethics-driven thinking. The flip side of all this wandering, however, is that I lack desired depth in some of these areas, but I've managed to surround myself with great collaboraters to mostly make up for that.

Future work. Other things I have a deep interest in but have yet to write about: CS x ethics education, misaligned incentives in academia, the excessiveness of utilitarian and meritocratic thinking rooted in our culture... and more. I plan to get to these themes eventually when time allows.

Outside work. avid reader, boxer (cal boxing '18), photog. warriors basketball fan. housing/transit policy enthusiast. I also occassionally help some friends run a local Indian food popup—you can help by following @curryinahurrynyc!
